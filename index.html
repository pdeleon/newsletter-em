Speeding Through Gains

Ford (F -1.14%) kicked off August in style.

The company was up 5% on Monday before taking slight losses on Tuesday.

But July is what the company is most excited about:

  • July was Ford’s best month since 2008.
  • The automaker gained 32% in July.
  • Ford raised its dividend back to pre pandemic levels ($0.15 per share).

What drove Ford? The stock’s performance was driven by innovative product announcements and a solid second quarter earnings report.

Tis’ The Season For Earnings

Uber (UBER -18.90%) reported second-quarter losses yesterday, but made up for it in revenue.

  • Losses: $1.33 per share.
  • Revenue: $8.07 billion, much higher than the $7.39 billion estimate.

Uber had a boost in business for both its transportation and delivery business.

  • Travel: $13.4 billion, a 57% increase year over year.
  • Deliveries: $13.9 billion, a 12% increase year over year.

While U.S. store sales flourished during Q2, its performance in China, its second biggest market, saw a 44% decline in sales.

Despite the trouble in China, the coffee chain opened 318 new locations around the world last quarter, raising its global location count to 34,948.

Starbucks (SBUX -1.41%) also reported mixed earnings yesterday.

  • Earnings: Gained 84 cents per share.

Try Not to Worry

Batten down the hatches!

Americans are bracing for a recession.

The survey says: 66% of Americans worry that a major recession is around the corner.

  • Last year at this time; only 48% of Americans felt that way.

The main reason people are scared about a recession is high inflation. As prices increase, Americans take a pay cut because virtually everything becomes more expensive.

71% said their wages are not keeping pace with rising expenses.

General tips: You could ramp up your savings and cut back on spending - those are typically good ways to limit the impact of an economic downturn.

Think outside the box; find a side hustle, invest in alternatives, drink less Starbucks!

Workers Unwanted

June’s job openings were at the lowest level since September.

  • Total vacancies dropped to 10.7 million, a 605,000 decline from the last reading.
  • There are still about 1.8 job openings per available worker right now, for a total of 4.8 million positions.

Hiring also slowed down by 2% to 6.37 million during the month, with the unemployment rate hanging tight at 3.6%.

China’s Economy is in Trouble

Land sales make up a huge chunk of China’s yearly revenue.

Unfortunately for them, land sales have fallen 30% this year.

The loss in confidence in China’s property sector could further drag down the Chinese economy.

Here are a few major issues the market is dealing with:

  • China Evergrande Group (Chinese real estate company) failed to secure a massive $300 billion restructuring plan last weekend.
  • Homeowners in 22 Chinese cities are refusing to pay their loans on unfinished housing projects.

On top of that, China could miss its 5.5% GDP growth target for the year.

Air Travel Chaos (Europe)

In the latest attempt to calm down air travel chaos in Europe, British Airways has suspended short-haul ticket sales from London's Heathrow Airport.

Heathrow Airport has recently experienced delays to 40% of its flights.

Other measures: Amsterdam's Schiphol Airport has extended its summer passenger cap. The maximum number of passengers departing in September will be limited to 67,500.

  • The passenger cap was originally introduced at the airport in June. Heathrow Airport also has a passenger cap (limited to 100,000 departures per day) until September 11th.

What’s to blame? Higher than expected demand for travel and a shrinking labor market. This perfect storm has left fewer employees to deal with higher demand.

Drowning In Debt

Household debt surpassed $16 trillion for the first time ever in Q2, with mortgage debt causing the most damage.

  • The total household debt was measured at $16.15 trillion, a $312 billion increase from Q1.
  • Mortgage debt surged the most, increasing by $207 billion to $11.4 trillion.

Why so high? Americans are borrowing record-high amounts as inflation pushes up the cost of housing. The average price of a home in the U.S. is now at $428,000, a 30% price hike since 2020.

Free-For-All

Hackers stole $200 million worth of crypto from the crypto start-up Nomad.

Nomad is a bridge platform that allows users to transfer tokens from other blockchains.

How’d they do it? The hackers allegedly found a coding error in Nomad’s recent upgrade, and were able to withdraw an abundance of tokens whenever users initiated a transfer - The attack is being referred to as a “free for all”.

Nomad has not said if users will be reimbursed or if they have any main suspects.
